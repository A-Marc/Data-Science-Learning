
Bayes' Theorem — Summary

What is Bayes' Theorem?
Bayes' Theorem is a fundamental formula in probability theory that allows us to update our beliefs about an event based on new evidence. It calculates the probability of a cause given an observed effect.

The Formula:
P(A|B) = [P(B|A) * P(A)] / P(B)

Where:
- P(A|B) = Posterior probability (probability of A given B has occurred)
- P(B|A) = Likelihood (probability of observing B given A is true)
- P(A) = Prior probability (initial probability of A before observing B)
- P(B) = Marginal probability (total probability of observing B)

Alternative Form (using Law of Total Probability):
P(A|B) = [P(B|A) * P(A)] / [P(B|A) * P(A) + P(B|A') * P(A')]

Where A' is the complement of A (not A).

Intuition:
Bayes' Theorem answers: "If I observe B, what's the probability that A caused it?"
It reverses conditional probabilities: if you know P(B|A), you can find P(A|B).

Key Components Explained:
1. Prior P(A): What we believed before seeing any evidence
2. Likelihood P(B|A): How well our hypothesis A explains the evidence B
3. Posterior P(A|B): Our updated belief after seeing the evidence
4. Marginal P(B): How likely the evidence is overall (normalization factor)

Classic Example: Medical Testing
A disease affects 1% of the population. A test is 95% accurate (detects disease when present) and has a 5% false positive rate.
If you test positive, what's the probability you actually have the disease?

Let:
- D = has disease, P(D) = 0.01
- + = tests positive
- P(+|D) = 0.95 (true positive rate)
- P(+|D') = 0.05 (false positive rate)

Using Bayes' Theorem:
P(D|+) = [P(+|D) * P(D)] / [P(+|D) * P(D) + P(+|D') * P(D')]
P(D|+) = [0.95 * 0.01] / [0.95 * 0.01 + 0.05 * 0.99]
P(D|+) = 0.0095 / [0.0095 + 0.0495]
P(D|+) = 0.0095 / 0.059
P(D|+) ≈ 0.161 or 16.1%

Surprising result: Even with a positive test, there's only a 16.1% chance of having the disease!
This is because the disease is rare (low prior), so false positives outnumber true positives.

Another Example: Email Spam Filter
- 60% of emails are spam, P(Spam) = 0.6
- Word "FREE" appears in 80% of spam emails, P(FREE|Spam) = 0.8
- Word "FREE" appears in 10% of legitimate emails, P(FREE|Not Spam) = 0.1
- What's P(Spam|FREE)?

P(Spam|FREE) = [P(FREE|Spam) * P(Spam)] / [P(FREE|Spam) * P(Spam) + P(FREE|Not Spam) * P(Not Spam)]
P(Spam|FREE) = [0.8 * 0.6] / [0.8 * 0.6 + 0.1 * 0.4]
P(Spam|FREE) = 0.48 / [0.48 + 0.04]
P(Spam|FREE) = 0.48 / 0.52 ≈ 0.923 or 92.3%

If an email contains "FREE", there's a 92.3% chance it's spam.

Real-World Applications:
1. Medical Diagnosis: Interpreting test results and symptoms
2. Machine Learning: Naive Bayes classifiers for text classification, spam detection
3. Search Engines: Ranking and relevance scoring
4. Risk Assessment: Finance, insurance, fraud detection
5. A/B Testing: Updating beliefs about which variant is better
6. Robotics: Sensor fusion and localization

Common Pitfalls:
1. Ignoring the base rate (prior probability) — leads to base rate fallacy
2. Confusing P(A|B) with P(B|A) — these are generally different!
3. Forgetting to calculate P(B) using the law of total probability
4. Misunderstanding what "95% accurate" means in context

Relationship to Conditional Probability:
Bayes' Theorem is derived from the definition of conditional probability:
- P(A|B) = P(A and B) / P(B)
- P(B|A) = P(A and B) / P(A)
- Therefore: P(A and B) = P(A|B) * P(B) = P(B|A) * P(A)
- Rearranging: P(A|B) = [P(B|A) * P(A)] / P(B)

Key Takeaway:
Bayes' Theorem is a powerful tool for reasoning under uncertainty. It shows how to rationally update beliefs when new evidence arrives, making it essential for data science, machine learning, and statistical inference.
